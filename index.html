Intro

I am a postdoctoral research assistant at the Johns Hopkins University, working with the Extrasolar Planetary Systems Imaging Group (EPSIG) from the Space Telescope Science Institute. I graduated with my Ph.D. from Sorbonne University in September 2023 under the supervision of Dr. Anthony Boccaletti and Dr. Benjamin Charnay at the LESIA Laboratoire d’études spatiales et d’instrumentation en astrophysique in Paris Observatory (now the LIRA Laboratoire d’études spatiales et d’instrumentation en astrophysique).

My research is focused on the study of exoplanetary systems : giant exoplanets, their atmospheres and their environments. My former and recent work has focused mainly on using direct imaging with the James Webb Space Telescope Mid-InfraRed Instrument MIRI. I am part of the Guaranteed Time Observation (GTO) ExoMIRI team, the European GTO program dedicated to the study of exoplanets and brown dwarfs. I also joined the Early Release Science (ERS) program 1386, focused on high-contrast imaging of exoplanets and exoplanetary systems with JWST, as well as the GTO Telescope Science Team (TST) led at STScI. My involvement extends across multiple JWST observing programs, and I am the co-PI of the GO 3647 program.

Implementing cross-correlation methods to study exoplanetary atmospheres of giant planets.

  • Simulation and preparatory work for JWST observations.

    • The research topic of my Ph.D. was to develop and evaluate the potential of cross-correlation methods, often referred as the molecular mapping method for the Medium Resolution Spectrograph of the instrument MIRI on JWST. The MRS is an integral field spectrograph with a spectral resolution of between 1500 and 3700. Although this instrument was not originally designed for high-contrast imaging, our work demonstrated the potential of this mode of observation for characterizing the atmospheres of exoplanets. The MRS allows, for the first time, to measure spectro-imaging data at wavelengths longer than 5 μm, making it especially valuable for studying exoplanetary atmospheres and especially the coldest ones, which emit most of their flux at these wavelengths. The molecular mapping method consists of cross correlating each spectral pixel from the spectral data cube with a planetary atmosphere model. The correlation factor obtained at each position is used to reconstruct a 2D correlation map whose value will be maximal at the position of a planet, provided that stellar diffraction residuals and background noise are not correlated with the model. This technique makes it possible to detect exoplanets, identify the molecules present in their atmospheres (by correlating with a spectral model of a specific molecule) and thus constrain atmospheric parameters, whereas direct extraction of the planet’s spectra from the data would be severely limited by stellar residuals. Using simulated MRS data, we have shown that this method is even more effective for characterizing the atmospheres of cold exoplanets because the spectral signatures due to molecules are more numerous. The results indicate that the method is promising for detecting various molecules (H₂O, CO, NH₃, CH₄, HCN, PH₃, CO₂), depending on the angular separation between the star and the planet, the depth of spectral signatures, and the planet's brightness. Our study identified few most promising targets for observation, and the parametric study helps anticipate the ability to characterize planets that will be discovered in the future.
